Coarse-grained modelling#

Fundamentals of Coarse-Grained Trajectories#

Note

  • Understand the coarse-grained mapping between All-Atoms (AA) systems and coarse-grained (CG) systems.

  • Use a mapping function to convert an AA trajectory to a CG trajectory

  • a bead: a partilce in CG model (a group of atoms)

In MD simulations:

  • Trajectory: a time series of the coordinates of partiles

  • Frame: The coordinates of particles at a certain time point (each frame represents a microstate)

  • The basic data set in a trajectory frame includes thermodynamic parameters (pressure, temperature, size of box,…) and a \(N \times D\) matrix, where \(N\) is the number of particles and \(D\) is the number dimensions present for each particle (coordinates, velocities, forces, per-atom features,…)

What is a coarse-grained (CG) model? [1]

A CG model is used to describe a particle system with a lower resolution than a finer-grained (often atomistic) model. Usually, each particle (or bead) in a CG model represents a group of multiple atoms in the corresponding atomistic model. Equivalently, a group of atoms in an all-atom system are mapped to a single CG site. Several examples of CG mappings are given below.


Atomic (a) and Coarse-grained (b) structures of the EMIM+NO3- ionic liquid.


The four-site CG model of actin subunit (right) in a filament (left).


All-atom and CG models of membrane lipid molecules with different resolutions (from high to low).

there are multiple choices of possible mappings for the same all-atom system (last figure). Consider a protein as an example: the CG representation can be at a relatively higher resolution (e.g., mapping one residue to one CG site) or lower resolution (e.g., mapping one sub-domain to one CG site). Understanding which mapping is the most appropriate is critical for CG modeling and requires domain specific knowlege.

Simulations#

Coarse-grained models are presently often used in combination with reconstruction tools [5]. Atomistic resolution models alone are presently not efficient enough to handle large system sizes and simulation timescales.

Steps to generate CG model: [3]

  1. Start with the target atomistic trajectory (may need equilirium AA model or just generate coords)

  2. Perform AA-to-CG mapping (“forward mapping”)

    • Transform sets of atomistic coordinates into CG ones simply by finding the center of mass of each group of atoms in a CG bead (Coarse-graining the atomistic trajectory)

  3. Simulation with CG model

  4. Backmapping CG-to-AA [6]

    • Due to the reduction of degrees of freedom involved in coarse-graining, the reverse process is not uniquely defined: there will be multiple atomistic configurations that can correspond to a CG grain structure.

    • Start with an arbitrary all-atoms positions (with COM of beads satifies CG positions), we perform minimize (or equilibrate) with some constraint (beads’ positions). Then we can get a consistent fine-grain configuration compatible with the starting CG configuration.

    • Need a backmapping tool [7]
